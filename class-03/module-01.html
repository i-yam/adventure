The
spine

You have a notebook full of interviews. In Class 04 the room gives you five minutes to turn it into something they will believe. Five minutes is not much. So you do not improvise it.

Every pitch that has ever worked is the same six boxes, filled in order. Miss one and the room feels the hole even if they cannot name it. Here they are.

Box one is the problem itself. State it so plainly that a stranger nods before you have said a word about what you are building. The fix comes later. Right now the room only wants to know the problem is real.

Box two is who has it. A problem everyone has is a problem nobody pays to fix. Name one person, specific enough that you could picture them sitting in the room.

Box three is the evidence. This is where the last two weeks of fieldwork get spent. What did you actually hear. Real sentences from real people beat any adjective you could reach for.

Box four is the hypothesis. Your guess at a solution, and you say the word guess out loud. You have no customers yet. Pretending otherwise is the fastest way to get caught.

Box five is the team. For a rough pitch this is short. Why you. Why you will still be doing this when it stops being fun.

Box six is why now. What changed. A cost that fell, a habit that shifted, a rule that lifted. If nothing changed, the room wonders why this problem waited around for you.

The work · 45 minutes
[ NO AI ] DO THIS ONE YOURSELF

Fill all six boxes for your own idea. One index card each, or one line each, no more. If a box takes a paragraph, you do not understand it yet. Keep the wording ugly and true. You will make it a story next.

The first draft of a pitch is where you find out whether you actually believe it. Hand that to a model and you walk into the room carrying someone else's conviction.

Reading
Paul Graham · Startup = Growth

Graham's answer to what makes a startup a startup, and why box six is really a question about how big and how fast. Read it before Class 04.

Deep dive: when the growth frame is wrong for you optional

Graham writes from inside YC, so his startup means one thing, a company built to grow very fast on other people's money. Plenty of good businesses are not that. A profitable two-person studio that grows ten percent a year is a fine life and a terrible venture pitch.

If that is what you are building, box six changes. You are not selling a rocket. You are selling a machine that prints a little more each year, reliably, for a long time. Know which one you have before the room decides for you.
