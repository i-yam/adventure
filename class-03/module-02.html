The
story

Here is a sentence I have heard forty times. "We are a platform that leverages AI to optimize the way people connect." Ten words, zero meaning. The room stopped listening at leverages.

You have six true boxes from the last module. True is a long way from told. Before you arrange anything, you need one sentence.

One sentence. What you do, and why it has to be you. Say it to someone who has never heard of your idea and watch their face. If they get it, you have your opening line. If they tilt their head, write it again. Ten tries is normal. The one that lands is usually shorter than the nine before it.

Now the boxes. On a slide they can sit in any order. Spoken, they cannot. A story moves. Problem, then the person it happens to, then the moment it got real for them, then your guess, then why you. The evidence gets woven in where it makes someone lean forward, not dropped in a pile. Same six boxes. One of them is now a beginning, one is an end.

The work · 15 minutes
[ NO AI ] DO THIS ONE YOURSELF

Write your one sentence. Then write it nine more times. Keep the tenth. Take your six boxes and put them in the order you would say them out loud to a stranger at a bar, not the order they sit on a slide. Read the whole thing aloud once, and time it.

The sentence that finally says what you mean is one you have to find by saying it wrong nine times. A model hands you the smooth version first, and smooth is how you say nothing.

Deep dive: the "for X who Y" template, and when to drop it optional

There is an old template for the one sentence. For a specific person who has a specific problem, we do the thing, unlike the current bad option. It is a good crutch when you are stuck, because it forces you to name the person and the alternative in the same breath.

It is a bad final answer, because every deck that leans on it sounds like every other deck that leans on it. Fill it in to find your parts. Then throw the scaffolding away and say the thing like a human.
