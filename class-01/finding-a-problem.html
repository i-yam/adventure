CLASS 01 · BUILD · FINDING A PROBLEM

Plausible is a trap.

01
INTEREST customers / COMFORT no-code / TIME 45min / TRACK core / FEEDS class 04 pitch
Finding
a problem

Look at the meme. The fool and the genius agree. The one crying in the middle has the MBA, the five years of consulting, and the whole TED shelf, and he has built nothing.

Both ends of that curve know the same four words. Make something people want. It is Y Combinator's whole motto, printed on the back of the hoodie. The trap is the middle, where you can read for a year, stack the credentials, and never once find out whether a single person wants the thing.

The idea that sounds good to everyone in the room is usually the one nobody has checked. Plausible and wanted are different things, and the only way to tell them apart is to go and ask. Asking has a method.

Steve Blank named it customer development, and it flips the usual order. Most people build the product first and go looking for customers after. Blank's move is to search for the customer first and build second.

The search has two steps, and you are not allowed near the company until both pass. Discovery: get out of the building and find a problem someone actually has. Validation: prove that same someone will hand over money or an hour of their week to make it go away. If the guess is wrong, you do not push forward. You pivot, back to discovery, and run it again. Only when the search returns a real, repeatable yes do you cross into the right half of the diagram and start building the machine and the company around it.

The left half is cheap and the right half is expensive. Everyone wants to skip to the right half. That is the disease the meme is laughing at.

Three companies you have heard of ran the left half honestly. None of them started with a finished product.

Drew Houston built the first Dropbox for himself, after leaving his USB stick at home one too many times. Then, instead of finishing it, he stopped and tested the guess. He posted a short screencast showing how the syncing would work, before the product was really built, and the beta waitlist went from 5,000 to 75,000 overnight. He had his answer before he wrote the hard part. Eric Ries tells it in The Lean Startup.

Airbnb was making $200 a week and going nowhere. Graham told the founders to go to their users, which sounded absurd, because meeting people one by one would never scale. They flew to New York anyway and knocked on doors. The idea was fine. The photographs were the problem, grainy phone shots that made decent apartments look like crime scenes, so they borrowed a camera and shot the listings themselves. Bookings moved. Paul Graham tells it in Do Things That Don't Scale.

Rahul Vohra refused to guess whether Superhuman had fit. He asked every user one question, how would you feel if you could no longer use this, and counted the ones who answered "very disappointed." Sean Ellis had found that companies with real traction almost always cleared 40 percent on that question. Superhuman started at 22, under the line, and Vohra built an engine to climb it. His write-up is in First Round Review.

The work · 45 minutes
[ NO AI ] DO THIS ONE YOURSELF

Write down three problems you personally ran into this week. Not startup ideas. Problems. The thing that annoyed you, cost you time, or made you mutter "why is this still like this." Pick the one you would most want gone. Then find five people who hit the same problem and ask them about it, without pitching anything. You are not selling. You are checking whether the problem is real and whether it is yours to chase. Bring what you find to the Class 04 pitch.

A model will brainstorm a hundred plausible problems in a second, and every one of them is the midwit's problem, not yours. Class 01 is about noticing something only you have standing to notice. Outsource that and you walk into the shark tank with nothing.

Reading
Paul Graham · How to Get Startup Ideas

Where real ideas come from, and why the ones that sound plausible to everyone are the traps. Read it before Class 02.

Deep dive: talk to users, or pick the right market? optional

Customer development says the answer is in the building across the street, so get out of yours and go find it. Marc Andreessen says something that sits badly next to that. In his telling the market is the only thing that matters, and a great team shipping a great product into a market that does not want it loses every time. The market pulls the product out of the startup, or it does not.

Both are right, and the seam between them is where founders get hurt. You can run flawless customer development inside a market that is too small to matter. You interview your five people, nail their problem, build a tidy thing for a room that never gets any bigger. Talking to users tells you whether the problem is real. It does not tell you whether the market is large enough to be worth your twenties. Blank teaches you to find the problem. Andreessen reminds you that some problems, solved perfectly, still do not add up to anything. Hold both. Read Andreessen's The Only Thing That Matters and argue with it.
