CLASS 01 · BUILD · COMPETITION

Most of your competition never shows up

01
INTEREST mindset / COMFORT no-code / TIME 45min / TRACK core / FEEDS class 04 pitch
The
lie

A thousand people want the exact thing you want. Picture them in a room. Now watch the room empty.

Most never stand up. Standing up means maybe failing in front of everyone, so they stay in their seats and call it being realistic. That is most of the room gone, and you have not done anything yet.

Of the ones who stand, most sit back down after the third time it doesn't work. A few keep going but at a pace that isn't going anywhere, a site they'll finish someday, an email they'll send next week. A few more had it and then chased something shinier. Look at the front of the room now. It's you and maybe one other person. That is the competition you were afraid of.

That picture comes from Shaan Puri's one-minute blog, and it lands because it's true. The word "competitive" counts everyone who wants the thing. Reality only runs on the people who show up and keep showing up. Those two numbers differ by about a thousand.

Charlie Munger had a line for what wins a thinned-out room. Take a simple idea and take it seriously. Not a clever idea. A simple one, taken seriously, which almost nobody actually does.

Peter Thiel says it with more swagger. Competition is for losers. In Zero to One he splits business into two kinds, the monopoly and the crowd, with almost nothing between them. In the crowd, everyone sells the same thing at the same price, and the price gets bid down until nobody makes money. Airlines move millions of people a year and earn close to nothing. Google runs one search engine and prints money. So his advice is to get out of the crowd. Find or build the market nobody else is in, start small enough to own it, then widen it.

Notice who that advice serves. You, the founder, and nobody else in the frame. Read it from any other seat and it turns sour. The price getting bid down until nobody makes money is the price dropping for the person buying. The margin Thiel wants you to protect is money the customer keeps when there's a rival across the street. Competition is the best thing capitalism does. It's why coffee is cheaper and better than it was ten years ago, why the phone in your pocket keeps improving, why the lazy incumbent eventually loses. It's the pressure that turns good enough into good. A monopoly is somebody who found a way to switch that pressure off and keep the difference. Thiel calls that winning. From the back of the line it has a worse name.

He's right about the mechanics, which is what makes the slogan stick. A startup whose only edge is being a little cheaper will die, because someone can always be a little cheaper. The honest version of his point is small. Be different enough to matter. That's a long way from competition is for losers, and the distance between them is where the damage gets done. Handed to a nineteen-year-old, it teaches you to treat the best part of the whole system as a problem to escape.

You probably won't build a monopoly anyway. A taco place on a street full of taco places is perfect competition by Thiel's own definition, and a good one still fills every table. It wins the plain way, by being the one that's serious about the tacos. The street full of rivals is the reason those tacos are any good. You do not need a market no one else is in. You need to be the one in the room who showed up.

Then there's the market that looks wide open. Sometimes nobody's there because nobody has figured it out yet. More often nobody's there because people tried and there was no money in it. Empty rooms are usually empty for a reason. Find the reason before you fall in love.

The work · 45 minutes
[ AI OK ] USE THE TOOLS

Take the problem you're chasing for the Class 04 pitch. You are going to build the real list of who's actually competing, then cut it down to the people who count.

Start with a scan. Ask a model to find everyone solving this problem, and push it for specifics rather than a vague paragraph:

"I want to build [your idea in one line]. List every existing product, service, or business a normal person might use instead. For each one, give me the name, what it actually does, roughly how big it is, and when it last shipped anything. Flag any you're not sure still exists."

Now do the part the model can't. Open each name. Which ones still exist? Which have real users, real reviews, a pulse in the last year? Cross off the dead, the abandoned, the landing page with no product behind it, the enterprise tool a normal person would never touch. What's left is your actual competition, and it will be a far shorter list than the scan handed you.

The model lies in a specific direction here. It pads the list to look thorough. It names companies that folded, invents one or two that never existed, and describes a dead product in the present tense because its training data remembers it alive. It inflates your competition the same way fear does. Your job is to be the one who checks.

AI-on for this one because a founder really does start with a scan. Doing it by hand would be slower and no wiser. The skill isn't getting the list. It's cutting it.

Reading
Peter Thiel · Competition Is for Losers

The monopoly argument in his own words, from the YC startup class. Watch it, then argue with it.

Deep dive: the empty market and the two lies optional

Thiel's sharper point is about how founders describe their market, and both directions are lies. The company with a lot of competitors shrinks its market until it sounds unique. We're the only farm-to-table Korean place near the station. Slice the categories thin enough and anyone sounds like a monopoly, right up until dinner, when you're fighting every restaurant in town for the same appetite. The company with a real monopoly does the reverse. It swears it's one small player in a giant market, so nobody notices how much it owns. When you read a competitor scan, notice which lie you're being told.

Then the empty market. The reason to be nervous about open space is that markets are efficient about obvious money. If a lot of people would pay for a thing and it were easy, someone would already be selling it. So when you find a room with nobody in it, ask which kind it is. New space, opened by something that just changed, a new tool, a new law, a new habit. Or dead space, where the graveyard is just out of frame. The good open markets almost always have a reason they opened recently that wasn't true two years ago. If you can't name the reason, assume it's a graveyard.
