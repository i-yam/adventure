The
brief

A venture fund makes ten bets. Seven die. Two return the money. One pays for the building, the salaries, and the next ten bets.

The partners knew that going in. They watched seven of their own picks fail and they signed the checks anyway. Not because they enjoy losing. Because they did the arithmetic. When one win can be a hundred times the size of a loss, betting on a single sure thing is the reckless move, not the safe one.

Last class you went outside and found a problem. Maybe you found three. By now you have a favorite, and you have started to defend it. That is the thing to watch.

A dead idea costs you a week. A favorite you married in week two costs you the term. The room in Class 04 will not care which idea you loved. It will ask whether the problem is real and whether it is big. So today you stop pitching one idea and start managing a slate.

The work · 45 minutes
[ AI OK ] USE THE TOOLS

Open a blank doc. Write down every problem you noticed outside the building, not only the one you fell for. The two you dismissed as too small. The one that annoyed you. The one you assumed someone must have already solved. Push the list to ten. A slate of one is a crush with a spreadsheet.

Now make a model argue with you about size. Paste your list into a prompt like this one:

I'm evaluating startup problems by how big each one could get. For every problem below, estimate its total addressable market, the number of people or businesses who have the problem multiplied by what they already pay to work around it, and its CAGR, the rate that market is growing each year. State every assumption in plain sight and flag where you are guessing rather than counting. Then rank the problems from largest opportunity to smallest. [paste your ten problems]

Those two words follow you the rest of the course, so learn them here. The total addressable market, the TAM, is the whole prize: everyone who has the problem and the money they would spend to make it go away. The CAGR is how fast that prize is growing, compounded, year on year. A big TAM growing at a healthy CAGR is the room's favorite sentence, and you will say it in Class 04. You already met your TAM. In Class 01 you found one person with the problem. The TAM is that person, counted, times everyone else who is them.

Read the output like a skeptic, because the model will hand you a confident TAM for all ten and mean none of them. A market it invents at forty billion dollars is worth exactly as much as its worst assumption, and the CAGR is where it lies quietest, a clean growth rate for a market it cannot actually see. Go find the assumption under each. When it calls a TAM huge, ask who is paying for the workaround today. When it cannot name a buyer, or a reason the market is growing, the number is decoration. Cross those out.

Then rank what survives, and rank it by the wrong-feeling rule: not which problem you like, but which could be enormous if you are right. The biggest ones tend to be the problems that looked like too much work, the ones everyone walks past because solving them is a schlep. That is not luck. The unglamorous problem has less competition for the simple reason that everyone else also found it unglamorous and left.

The sizing is grunt work a model does in seconds and you would do badly in an hour. Your job starts after it answers: catching the number when it lies.

So now you have a ranked slate. Which is a polite way of saying you still have not chosen. That comes next.
