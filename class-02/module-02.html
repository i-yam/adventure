CLASS 02 · BUILD · WHEN TO STOP LOOKING

Stop looking. Pick one.

02
INTEREST mindset / COMFORT no-code / TIME 15min / TRACK core / FEEDS class 04 pitch
The
brief

You have a ranked list of problems now. You could keep adding to it forever. Plenty of founders do, right up to the day the money runs out and they are left with a very tidy list and no company.

The hard part was never finding options. It is closing the search and living with the one you picked. A slate is where founders go to hide from the choosing. There is a math to when you stop, and it is older than any startup.

The work · 15 minutes
[ NO AI ] DO THIS ONE YOURSELF

You are flat-hunting in a city where the good places get taken the same afternoon you see them, and you cannot go back to one you passed on. Take the place you love on the first day and you have no idea whether it is any good, because you have seen nothing to measure it against. Wait until you have seen every place in town and the good ones went weeks ago. There is a known answer to this. Look at the first thirty-seven percent of what you expect to see and take none of it. Just learn what good looks like. Then sign for the first place that beats everything in that opening batch. That is the whole rule, and it lands near one over e, if you enjoy that sort of thing.

This has a name, the secretary problem, and it comes out of Algorithms to Live By, the book a lot of this course is built on. The same math is why people only half-joke about a rule for when to stop dating and settle down. Apartments, hires, co-founders, a person to spend your life with, a problem for your startup: any time you see the options one at a time, cannot go back, and cannot see the whole field at once, thirty-seven percent is the answer to when you quit looking.

Your slate is the same problem in a suit. You have been looking since Class 01. The fieldwork, and the ten problems you sized, were your thirty-seven percent. The looking is over. You know what a big problem looks like now, which you did not two weeks ago.

Set the bar at the best problem you have seen so far and write that bar down in one line. Then take your ranked slate from the top and walk down it. The first problem that clears the bar and that you could start on this week is the one. Circle it. Stop walking. Do not read the rest of the list hoping something better is further down. Something better, further down, is exactly what the rule is protecting you from.

The problem you circle is the one you carry into the Class 04 pitch. It will not be the one you liked most walking in here. Every extra week you spend still looking is a week the good option in front of you goes cold, and a switch made late costs far more than one made early. The looking was free in Class 01. It is expensive now.

A model will rank your options all day and never once tell you to stop. Knowing when to quit looking is a bet on a future nobody has seen yet, and that call is yours. Hand it off and you never learn to make it.

You have a problem now. One. Written down, circled, committed. Which is the most dangerous your idea will ever be, because now you believe it.
