CLASS 02 · BUILD · END IT BEFORE YOU FALL FOR IT

One loud yes is not a market.

02
INTEREST customers · mindset / COMFORT no-code / TIME 15min / TRACK core / FEEDS class 04 pitch
The
brief

There is always one. One person in your interviews who lit up, who said this is exactly the thing I have been waiting for, who gave you their number before you asked for it. You have been quoting them ever since. You will want to quote them in the pitch.

Last part you committed to one problem, the way the part before had you pick one apartment and, if you followed the thread, one person to look for. Commitment does something to your eyes. Give it a week and you stop seeing the thing straight. That one interview that lit up becomes the reason you wave away every shrug that came after.

That is overfitting, and it sinks more ideas than any competitor. A model bent to fit every bump in last quarter's numbers predicts next quarter badly, and you have just built your whole read on the one customer who bumped hardest. Fit yourself to your loudest fan and you get a story that delights five people and lands with nobody else.

One reaction means different things depending on what you believed walking in. If ninety people shrugged and one lit up, the light is probably noise. If everyone leaned in and one lit up brightest, the light is signal. Same data point, opposite reading. The prior does the work, so today you make the prior say its name.

The work · 15 minutes
[ NO AI ] DO THIS ONE YOURSELF

Write down the single strongest piece of evidence you have that your problem is real. The quote, the moment, the thing that made you commit. One line.

Now be honest about what you believed before that moment. Did you already want this to be true when you walked into the room? Write that down too. A founder who went in wanting a yes tends to come out having heard one.

Then run the evidence two ways. As a believer first: assume the problem is real, and ask whether the evidence fits. Of course it does. That is why you kept it. Now as a skeptic who is sure the problem is fake: could that exact same moment have happened anyway? A polite person being kind. One real outlier in a room of shrugs. If the moment looks identical whether the problem is real or fake, it proves nothing. You kept a feeling and stapled a quote to it.

Last, count the other side. The people who went lukewarm, who said cool and changed the subject, who never wrote back. That quiet is data, and it is the data founders throw out, because the yes is loud and the shrug is easy to forget. Put a number on the shrugs and set it next to your one bright light.

If your problem survives the skeptic, walk it into Class 04 stronger, because you already found the hole the room is going to dig at. If it does not survive, you found that out on a Tuesday.

A model will argue whichever side you point it at, and argue it well enough to talk you back into the thing you love. The whole exercise is catching your own bias, and you cannot hand that to the tool that already shares it.

End it here, quietly, if it needs ending. A breakup in week two costs you a weekend. The one on stage in Class 04 costs you the term.
